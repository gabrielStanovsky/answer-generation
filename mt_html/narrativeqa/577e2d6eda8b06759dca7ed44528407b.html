Task Instruction:

In this task, you are given a passage, a question, a correct answer, and a predicted answer. The task is to score how closely a predicted answer captures the same information (i.e. have the same meaning) as a correct answer using the passage, question, and human commonsense knowledge.

Scoring is on a scale from 1 to 5 .
A 1 score means the predicted answer captures none of the information of the correct answer, and thus is a completely wrong answer.
A 5 score means the predicted answer captures the same information as the correct answer, and thus is a completely right answer.



Note: A predicted answer may answer the question correctly, but to be given a score of 5, it must be equivalent to the correct answer. An example is provided below of this case.

Note: The score you give should not depend on the capitalization of the correct and predicted answer.

Note: A helpful tip for searching for the correct answer in the passage is using Control+F to search for it.

Examples (Sorted by Scores):

(Completely Right) Predicted answers that should recieve a 5:

  Some ways a predicted answer can get a score of 5.
  • It is clear that that the predicted and correct answers refer to the same person, place, or object.
  • The predicted and correct answers are synonyms.

Question: What is the relationship of Maria Wyndham to Mr. Fletcher?
Correct Answer: grandaughter
Predicted Answer: Maria is Mr. Fletcher's granddaughter.
Justification: The predicted answer captures the same information as the correct answer when taking into account the passage and question.

Passage: …The next day, two men, John and Jim (who are drug dealers), arrive at the apartment to pick up the package…
Question: Who comes to pick up the package the next day ?
Correct Answer: Drug Dealers
Predicted Answer: Jim and John
Justification: In this case, John and Jim are the drug dealers so this is completely correct. More generally, if the correct answer and the predicted answer both refer to the same entity, then the predicted answer is correct, even if it is written differently.

Passage: …Later, Tom and three other boys combine their money to buy a boat named Dolphin and sneak away to an island…
Question: Tom and three other friends put their money together to buy what?
Correct Answer: A boat
Predicted Answer: A boat named Dolphin
Justification: Similar to above, since "a boat named Dolphin" and "a boat" both refer to the same entity.

Passage: …Thrilling things happen at Monte Carlo…
Question: What kind of things happen in Monte Carlo?
Correct Answer: In Monte Carlo thrilling things happen.
Predicted Answer: Fun and exciting.
Justification: Fun and exciting is synonymous with "thrilling", and so we give this a 5. More generally, it is important to look out for synonyms when scoring answers.

Passage: Mr Hillard hears from his window on a night of fog and mist a young woman's voice singing divinely. Later on, Mr. Hillard again meets the young, adventurous singer under most mystifying circumstances. They dine together in New York…

Correct Answer: The Singer.
Predicted Answer: The lady from the window of the hotel in New York.
Justification: Similar to above, "the singer" and "the lady from the window" refer to the same person.

(Mostly Right) Predicted answers that should recieve a 4:

  A score of 4 means that there is a minor detail wrong with the predicted answer, but otherwise, is a perfect answer. Some ways a predicted answer can get a score of 4.
  • The predicted answer is a little ungrammatical.
  • The predicted answer is a little unspecific (e.g. the correct answer is the city but the predicted answer is the country of the city).
  • The predicted answer gets the gender wrong (e.g. he vs she, him vs her).
  • The predicted answer gets the plurality wrong (e.g. he vs they, policeman vs policemen).

(Half Right) Predicted answers that should recieve a 3:

  Some ways a predicted answer can get a score of 3.
  • The prediction gets half of the details right (see example 1).
  • The prediction gets the details right, but is quite unspecific (see example 2)
  • The correct answer is a list but the prediction only gets some of the elements of the list.
  • The correct answer is a single item (person, object, etc) but the prediction adds some extra items.
  • The predicted answer gets the first name right but the last name wrong (or vice versa).

  A score of 2 means that the predicted answer is incorrect, but there is some slight redeemable quality to it.
  • The predicted answer is clearly wrong, but there is some word overlap between the correct and predicted answer that makes it seem slighly correct and the predicted answer is grammatical. (See example 1)
  • The prediction gets one thing correct, but more than one thing wrong. (See example 2)
  • The predicted gets a detail wrong that significantly affects the meaning, but still gets some minor details correct.

  There are a number of ways a predicted answer can get a score of 1.
  • TRICKY!!! A predicted answer can technically be a correct answer to the question, but if it does not have the same meaning as the correct answer, it is a score of 1 for this task. (see example 1)
  • The predicted doesn't make any sense or is very ungrammatical.
  • The answer gets all details wrong (e..g the name(s) are completely wrong, places are completely wrong, etc).
  • The predicted answer is opposite of the correct answer (e.g. yes vs no, hot vs cold, etc.)

Lulu herself is described as “the true animal, the wild, beautiful animal” and the “primal form of woman”. When the action of the play starts, Lulu has been rescued by the rich newspaper publisher Dr Schön from a life on the streets with her alleged father, the petty criminal Schigolch. Dr Schön has taken Lulu under his wing, educated her and made her his lover. Wishing however to make a more socially advantageous match for himself, he has married her off to the medic Dr Goll. In the first Act Dr Goll has brought Lulu to have her portrait painted by Schwarz. Left alone with him, Lulu seduces the painter. When Dr Goll returns to confront them, he collapses with a fatal heart attack. In Act Two, Lulu has married the painter Schwarz, who, with Schön’s assistance, has now achieved fame and wealth. She remains Schön’s mistress, however. Wishing to be rid of her ahead of his forthcoming marriage to a society belle, Charlotte von Zarnikow, Schön informs Schwarz about her dissolute past. Schwarz is shocked to the core and “guillotines” himself with his razor. In Act Three Lulu appears as a dancer in a revue, her new career promoted by Schön’s son Alwa, who is now also infatuated with her. Dr Schön is forced to admit that he is in her thrall. Lulu forces him to break off his engagement to Charlotte. In Act Four Lulu is now married to Dr Schön but is unfaithful to him with several other men (Schigolch, Alwa, the circus artist Rodrigo Quast and the lesbian Countess Geschwitz). On discovering this, Schön presses a revolver into her hand, urging her to kill herself. Instead, she uses it to shoot Schön, all the while declaring him the only man she has ever loved. She is imprisoned for her crime. Her escape from prison with the aid of Countess Geschwitz and subsequent career down to her death at the hands of Jack the Ripper in London are the subject of the sequel, Pandora’s Box. It is now customary in theatre performances to run the two plays together, in abridged form, under the title Lulu.
